How it works

Nothing leaves this machine. No servers, no accounts, no telemetry, no network calls of any kind.

Two storage modes. Local database keeps your collection in this browser's store (convenient, but plaintext on this host). Vault-only writes nothing to the browser — data lives in memory, you open and save encrypted .vault files, and closing the tab leaves no trace. Vault-only is the mode for running from a USB stick on a machine you don't want to leave data on.

Encryption. Encrypted vaults use AES-256-GCM. A random master key encrypts the data; your passphrase only wraps that master key (PBKDF2, 250,000 rounds). No third-party crypto. There is no backdoor and no password reset — lose the passphrase and the vault is unrecoverable by anyone.

Shard-ready by design. Because the passphrase wraps a master key rather than encrypting the data directly, that master key is a single secret that can later be split with Shamir's Secret Sharing (e.g. any 3 of 5 shares reconstruct it) for inheritance — without re-encrypting anything. That layer isn't built yet; the format is prepared for it.

If encryption is unavailable over file://, serve from localhost: python3 -m http.server 8080 → http://localhost:8080/. Still fully offline.

Documents. The insurance schedule and estate register open the print dialog — choose Save as PDF. The insurance schedule flags every watch whose market value exceeds its scheduled (insured) value.

Succession & recovery

The honest version first: there is no "reset password by email." That isn't an oversight — it's the security. An email reset is a backdoor, and a backdoor is exactly what an air-gapped encrypted vault exists to avoid. Any mechanism that could let you back in without the passphrase could let someone else in too. So the recovery question isn't "how does the app let heirs in" — the app can't and shouldn't. It's "where does a copy of the key live, and who is trusted to hold it."

The right mechanism for one person's collection is custody, not code:

— Write the passphrase on paper. Seal it. Place it where your estate is settled: a safe-deposit box, or with your estate attorney, or in a fireproof home safe your executor can reach. Reference its existence and location in your will (never write the passphrase itself in the will — wills become public record in probate).

— Keep a copy of an encrypted vault export with that sealed passphrase, plus a copy of this app file, so an heir has the data, the key, and the tool together.

— Fill in the Owner & succession panel: your executor and alternate contact, and a note describing where the sealed passphrase is held (the note, not the passphrase). That travels inside the encrypted vault.

The stronger mechanism, when you want it (the "sharded" option): split the key with Shamir's Secret Sharing — say 5 shares where any 3 reconstruct it — and hand shares to your executor, a trusted child, and your attorney. No single person can open the vault alone or be coerced into it, losing one share doesn't lock anyone out, and after your death the fiduciaries jointly reconstruct. This is how serious crypto custody handles inheritance. The vault format here is already built for it; the splitting tool is the next piece to add when you decide you want it.

What protects you while you're alive is not app cleverness: turn on full-disk encryption (FileVault / BitLocker), keep the vault and its passphrase in different places, and in Vault-only mode nothing plaintext is left behind on the host at all.
