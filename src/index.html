Questions & answers

Security
Is my watch collection actually safe in this app?

Yes. Everything the app stores on your computer is encrypted. Not hidden, not password-protected — encrypted. Without your passphrase it is unreadable to anyone, including us.

What does "encrypted" actually mean? Explain it simply.

Most people picture a locked filing cabinet. That is the wrong picture, because a filing cabinet can be pried open and then someone reads your papers.

This is different. Your collection is stored as scrambled nonsense. Someone who takes your laptop, pulls the drive out and reads the raw file gets random characters. There is no lock to pick, because the information itself is scrambled.

Your passphrase is the only thing that unscrambles it.

Who can see my collection?

You. That is the entire list.

Not us — there is no "us". No account, no server, no company holding a copy.

Not anyone on the internet. The app is built so it physically cannot make a network connection. Not "we promise not to" — the ability is switched off at the engine level.

Not someone using your unlocked laptop. The app locks itself, and locking throws away the key until you type your passphrase again.

Not Apple, not iCloud, not any backup service — unless you copy a file there yourself.

What happens if my laptop is stolen?

Whoever has it gets scrambled nonsense. Your watches, values, serial numbers, photos and documents are all inside the encrypted data, and it does not open without your passphrase.

This is the good case. Your computer is gone; your collection record is not exposed.

What if I forget my passphrase?

If you also lost your recovery key, your collection is gone. Permanently. Nobody can recover it — not you, not us, not anybody.

That sounds harsh, and it is the same fact that makes a stolen laptop survivable. Any back door that could let you back in could let someone else in too. So there isn't one. No email reset, no security questions, no support line.

What is the recovery key, and do I really need to write it down?

It is a one-time code shown once during setup, and it is the only other thing that can open your register. It is never shown again.

Yes, write it down. On paper. Put it where your important documents live — a safe, a safe-deposit box, with your attorney. Not in a note on the same laptop.

With the recovery key, a forgotten passphrase is a minor annoyance. Without either one, it is over.

What does this app NOT protect me from?

Being straight about the limits:

  • Malware running on your computer while your register is unlocked. If something has taken over your machine while you are logged in, encryption at rest does not help.
  • A passphrase written on a sticky note attached to the laptop.
  • Someone watching you type it.
  • You deleting your own data with no backup.

Encryption stops a file being read. It cannot stop the ordinary ways people lose things.

Do I have to take your word for any of this?

No, and you should not have to. The source code is public and readable. Anyone who can read code can verify every claim on this page. An app that asks you to take security on faith is not worth taking on faith.

What this app is for
What is this app actually for?

Five things. Most owners use two or three of them:

  • Insurance documentation
  • Knowing whether you are up or down, honestly
  • Estate planning
  • Tracking the hunt — grails and watches you are chasing
  • Simply knowing what you own
How does it help with insurance?

If a watch is stolen or lost, your insurer asks what you had, what it was worth, and what proof you have. Guessing after the fact is a bad position.

The app prints a scheduled property inventory — every watch, reference, serial number, market value and insured value — to hand to an insurer or attach to a claim. It flags anything worth more than it is insured for.

You can also attach the actual paperwork: invoices, warranty cards, appraisals, all encrypted alongside the record.

Does it tell me whether I am making or losing money?

Yes, and it keeps two numbers apart on purpose:

UNREALIZED — what your collection might be worth today, based on values you typed in. An estimate. Not money.

REALIZED — what actually happened when you sold. Real money, worked out properly: sale price, minus selling fees, minus what you paid, duties, shipping and servicing.

What does it do for estate planning?

The unglamorous part. If something happens to you, whoever handles your affairs needs to know what these watches are, what they are worth, which go to a named person and which can be sold.

The estate register prints exactly that, grouped by your instructions.

Can I track watches I do not own yet?

Yes. Mark a watch as "Seeking" and it sits in your register as part of the hunt — but it is excluded from every financial total and from both printed documents, because you do not own it.

When you buy it, enter the purchase price or date and the app converts it to Owned automatically.

Getting started
Why is it asking me for a passphrase the moment I open it?

Because everything it stores is encrypted, and that passphrase is the key. There is no "set this up later" — later would mean storing your collection unprotected in the meantime.

Pick something you will remember. Length matters more than complexity.

I already have my collection in a spreadsheet. Can I import it?

Yes. Data → Import from CSV. It reads common column headings and tolerates the usual spreadsheet mess.

It ADDS to your register — it never replaces what is already there — and it tells you how many rows it is about to add before doing anything.

Check the imported rows afterwards. It maps what it recognises; it cannot read your mind.

Do I have to fill in every field?

No. Maker and model are enough to save a watch. Everything else can be added whenever you get to it.

The fields that matter most for insurance are serial number, market value and scheduled (insured) value.

Recording your watches
There are three different rating fields. What is the difference?

They answer three different questions, and a watch can be all three at once:

STARS (1-5) — how much do I love this?
GRAIL — is this one of the ones I chased?
LEDGER RATING — what do I intend to do with it: Hold, Buy, Sell, Watch?

Why they are separate: a watch that has appreciated past what you would pay today can be five stars, a grail, and marked Sell all at the same time. One combined field could not say that.

What is the difference between "Grail" and "Holy grail"?

Grail is one you are evaluating, or one you chased and landed.

Holy grail is the unattainable one — the someday watch.

What does "Seeking" mean?

A watch you do not own yet, but are hunting or evaluating.

It shows in your register with a SEEKING tag, but never appears in a money total, an insurance schedule or an estate register. It is a placeholder for the hunt, not an asset.

What is the difference between "Sell" and "Sold"?

Ledger rating "Sell" is an intention. You still own it.

Status "Sold" is a fact. It is gone, it leaves your held totals, and it moves into your realized profit and loss.

Why did the app change a watch's status by itself?

On purpose, and it tells you when it happens.

Enter a sale date or price and it marks the watch Sold. Enter loss details and it marks it Lost. Enter purchase details on a Seeking watch and it marks it Owned.

Without this, a watch you had clearly sold would still be counted in your market value, cost basis and insurance coverage — making every total wrong with nothing on screen to warn you.

The money
Why is my "Realized" number lower than I expected?

Because it is the real one. It subtracts selling fees, duties and shipping you paid on the way in, and any servicing you logged while you owned it.

Worked example: a watch bought for $4,000, serviced twice for $1,950, sold for $6,500 with $780 in fees looks like a $1,570 win. It is actually a $380 loss. The disposition report shows both figures side by side.

Why does it say one of my watches is underinsured?

Its market value is higher than what your policy would pay out. The gap is what you would have to absorb yourself.

My policy pays more than the scheduled value. Can the app handle that?

Yes, but you have to tell it. Set the Insurer and "Pays up to" fields under Valuation & coverage.

Some policies pay above the scheduled value — Hodinkee pays up to 150%. Without that setting, the app assumes you are covered only for the scheduled figure and will warn you about gaps that do not exist.

It cuts the other way too. A standard homeowner's policy usually caps a single watch far below its value unless individually scheduled. Record that honestly and the app will tell you the truth.

One of my watches was stolen. How do I record it?

Set its status to Lost and fill in the Loss & claim section: the type of loss, the date, police report number, claim number, and what the insurer actually paid you.

The settlement is treated as proceeds, exactly like a sale price, so the claim appears properly in your realized profit and loss. The disposition report labels it as an insurance claim rather than a sale.

Photos and documents
What is the difference between a photo and a document?

PHOTOS are wrist shots and reference images. They are automatically shrunk so a page of twenty watches does not choke. Click any photo to see it full size, with a "Save full size" button.

DOCUMENTS are evidence — invoices, warranty cards, appraisals. Images or PDFs, stored exactly as you gave them, never compressed, because the fine print matters. They appear as a list on the watch's detail panel; click one to save the original file.

Use Documents for anything you might have to show an insurer.

How do I get a PDF of my insurance schedule?

Two steps:

  1. Data → Insurance schedule. This saves a file to your Downloads folder. That is the document itself, not a PDF yet.
  2. Open that file — it opens in your browser — press Cmd-P, and choose "Save as PDF" from the PDF menu at the bottom-left.

The app explains this on screen when you save the document.

Why doesn't the app just create the PDF directly?

Doing that would mean granting the app permission to write files anywhere on your computer and to launch other programs on your behalf. That is a real increase in what the app can reach, in exchange for saving you one click. It was not worth it.

What it deliberately does not do
Does it sync to my phone or to the cloud?

No. Your collection lives on this machine. Moving it to another computer means exporting an encrypted vault file and taking it there yourself.

That is the trade: no sync, but also nothing of yours sitting on somebody else's server.

Does it update market values automatically?

No. Market values are what you type in. A price feed would require a network connection, and this app does not make network connections.

Is there really no password reset?

Really. See "What if I forget my passphrase?" above. It is a deliberate feature, not a missing one.

Is any of this tax advice?

No. The app produces an accurate record of what you paid and what you got back. What that means for your taxes is a conversation with your accountant — collectibles are often treated differently from other assets.

When something looks wrong
I opened the app and my collection is gone. It wants me to set a new passphrase.

Stop. Do not create a new passphrase — that starts a fresh empty register.

Almost always this means the app is looking somewhere other than where your data is. Make sure the app is in your Applications folder, not being run from Downloads or from a mounted disk image. Move it there and reopen it.

Your data has not been deleted. The app is not finding it.

I forgot my passphrase. What now?

On the lock screen, choose "Use recovery key instead" and enter the recovery key from setup. Capitals, spaces and dashes do not matter.

Once you are in, change your passphrase to something memorable (Data → Change passphrase). Your recovery key keeps working.

If you have neither, see "What if I forget my passphrase?" above. There is genuinely nothing that can be done.

How do I back everything up?

Data → Save encrypted vault. That produces a single encrypted file holding everything: watches, photos, documents, owner details.

Keep it somewhere other than this laptop. It is encrypted, so a USB stick or a cloud drive is fine — without the passphrase it is unreadable to whoever holds it.

Two things worth doing:

  • Store the vault file and your recovery key in different places. Together in one folder means a single loss takes both.
  • Back up again after any significant change. The last backup is the only one that counts.
It said "Optimised N stored photos" when it opened. Is something wrong?

No, and it only happens once. An earlier version stored full-size camera images, which made the app slow. It is shrinking them for display. Files you attached under Documents are untouched.

How it works

A native desktop app. Watch Register runs as a real macOS and Windows application — not a website, not a browser tab. Nothing leaves this machine: no servers, no accounts, no telemetry, and no network calls of any kind. The app is built with its network access switched off at the engine level, so it cannot phone home even if something tried to.

Your collection is encrypted on disk. Everything the app stores on this machine is sealed with AES-256-GCM. There is no readable copy anywhere on your drive — not a database file, not a cache. When you open the app you are asked for your passphrase, and that passphrase is what decrypts the register into memory for the session. Close the app, lock it, or walk away long enough for the idle timer, and the decrypted copy is discarded.

Locking. The app locks on launch, and again after a period of inactivity you choose under Data → Security (15 minutes by default). You can also lock instantly with the Lock button in the header. Locking drops the decryption key, so a locked app is not a screen covering readable data — there is genuinely nothing to read until the passphrase is entered again.

What this does and doesn’t protect against. It protects against someone sitting down at your unlocked laptop, and against your drive or backups being read by someone who has the files but not your passphrase. It does not protect against malware running as you while the register is unlocked, and it cannot protect a passphrase you have written on a sticky note.

Passphrase and recovery key. A random master key encrypts your data. Your passphrase never encrypts the data directly — it only wraps that master key (PBKDF2-SHA256, 250,000 rounds). The same master key is wrapped a second time by the recovery key shown once during setup. Either one opens the register; neither can be derived from the other. Changing your passphrase re-wraps the key and takes an instant, because none of your data is re-encrypted.

There is no reset. No backdoor, no email recovery, no support channel that can let you back in. If you lose the passphrase and the recovery key, your collection is unrecoverable by anyone, permanently. Store the recovery key on paper, offline, where your estate documents live — see Succession & recovery.

Vault files. Alongside the encrypted local store you can export a portable .vault.json — the same envelope encryption in a single file you control. Use it for offsite backup, for moving between machines, or as the copy your executor receives. Vault-only mode writes nothing to this machine at all: the register lives in memory, you open and save vault files by hand, and quitting leaves no trace. That is the mode for a borrowed or shared computer.

Shard-ready by design. Because a passphrase wraps a master key rather than encrypting data directly, the same master key can carry additional wraps. The recovery key is the first of these. A future release can split that master key with Shamir’s Secret Sharing — any 3 of 5 shares, say, held by different family members — without re-encrypting anything you already have.

Documents. The insurance schedule and estate register open the print dialog — choose Save as PDF. The insurance schedule flags every watch whose market value exceeds its scheduled (insured) value.

Succession & recovery

The honest version first: there is no "reset password by email." That isn't an oversight — it's the security. An email reset is a backdoor, and a backdoor is exactly what an air-gapped encrypted vault exists to avoid. Any mechanism that could let you back in without the passphrase could let someone else in too. So the recovery question isn't "how does the app let heirs in" — the app can't and shouldn't. It's "where does a copy of the key live, and who is trusted to hold it."

The right mechanism for one person's collection is custody, not code:

— Write the passphrase on paper. Seal it. Place it where your estate is settled: a safe-deposit box, or with your estate attorney, or in a fireproof home safe your executor can reach. Reference its existence and location in your will (never write the passphrase itself in the will — wills become public record in probate).

— Keep a copy of an encrypted vault export with that sealed passphrase, plus a copy of this app file, so an heir has the data, the key, and the tool together.

— Fill in the Owner & succession panel: your executor and alternate contact, and a note describing where the sealed passphrase is held (the note, not the passphrase). That travels inside the encrypted vault.

The stronger mechanism, when you want it (the "sharded" option): split the key with Shamir's Secret Sharing — say 5 shares where any 3 reconstruct it — and hand shares to your executor, a trusted child, and your attorney. No single person can open the vault alone or be coerced into it, losing one share doesn't lock anyone out, and after your death the fiduciaries jointly reconstruct. This is how serious crypto custody handles inheritance. The vault format here is already built for it; the splitting tool is the next piece to add when you decide you want it.

What protects you while you're alive is not app cleverness: turn on full-disk encryption (FileVault / BitLocker), keep the vault and its passphrase in different places, and in Vault-only mode nothing plaintext is left behind on the host at all.
