================================================================
SECTION 1 — SECURITY
================================================================

Q1. Is my watch collection actually safe in this app?

A1. Yes. Everything the app stores on your computer is encrypted. Not hidden,
    not password-protected — encrypted. Without your passphrase it is unreadable
    to anyone, including us.


Q2. What does "encrypted" actually mean? Explain it simply.

A2. Most people picture a locked filing cabinet. That is the wrong picture,
    because a filing cabinet can be pried open and then someone reads your
    papers.

    This is different. Your collection is stored as scrambled nonsense. Someone
    who takes your laptop, pulls the drive out and reads the raw file gets
    random characters. There is no lock to pick, because the information itself
    is scrambled.

    Your passphrase is the only thing that unscrambles it.


Q3. Who can see my collection?

A3. You. That is the entire list.

    Not us — there is no "us". No account, no server, no company holding a copy.

    Not anyone on the internet. The app is built so it physically cannot make a
    network connection. Not "we promise not to" — the ability is switched off at
    the engine level.

    Not someone using your unlocked laptop. The app locks itself, and locking
    throws away the key until you type your passphrase again.

    Not Apple, not iCloud, not any backup service — unless you copy a file there
    yourself.


Q4. What happens if my laptop is stolen?

A4. Whoever has it gets scrambled nonsense. Your watches, values, serial
    numbers, photos and documents are all inside the encrypted data, and it does
    not open without your passphrase.

    This is the good case. Your computer is gone; your collection record is not
    exposed.


Q5. What if I forget my passphrase?

A5. If you also lost your recovery key, your collection is gone. Permanently.
    Nobody can recover it — not you, not us, not anybody.

    That sounds harsh, and it is the same fact that makes a stolen laptop
    survivable. Any back door that could let you back in could let someone else
    in too. So there isn't one. No email reset, no security questions, no
    support line.


Q6. What is the recovery key, and do I really need to write it down?

A6. It is a one-time code shown once during setup, and it is the only other
    thing that can open your register. It is never shown again.

    Yes, write it down. On paper. Put it where your important documents live —
    a safe, a safe-deposit box, with your attorney. Not in a note on the same
    laptop.

    With the recovery key, a forgotten passphrase is a minor annoyance. Without
    either one, it is over.


Q7. What does this app NOT protect me from?

A7. Being straight about the limits:

    - Malware running on your computer while your register is unlocked. If
      something has taken over your machine while you are logged in, encryption
      at rest does not help.
    - A passphrase written on a sticky note attached to the laptop.
    - Someone watching you type it.
    - You deleting your own data with no backup.

    Encryption stops a file being read. It cannot stop the ordinary ways people
    lose things.


Q8. Do I have to take your word for any of this?

A8. No, and you should not have to. The source code is public and readable.
    Anyone who can read code can verify every claim on this page. An app that
    asks you to take security on faith is not worth taking on faith.

SECTION 2 — WHAT THIS APP IS FOR
================================================================

Q9. What is this app actually for?

A9. Five things. Most owners use two or three of them:

    - Insurance documentation
    - Knowing whether you are up or down, honestly
    - Estate planning
    - Tracking the hunt — grails and watches you are chasing
    - Simply knowing what you own


Q10. How does it help with insurance?

A10. If a watch is stolen or lost, your insurer asks what you had, what it was
     worth, and what proof you have. Guessing after the fact is a bad position.

     The app prints a scheduled property inventory — every watch, reference,
     serial number, market value and insured value — to hand to an insurer or
     attach to a claim. It flags anything worth more than it is insured for.

     You can also attach the actual paperwork: invoices, warranty cards,
     appraisals, all encrypted alongside the record.


Q11. Does it tell me whether I am making or losing money?

A11. Yes, and it keeps two numbers apart on purpose:

     UNREALIZED — what your collection might be worth today, based on values you
     typed in. An estimate. Not money.

     REALIZED — what actually happened when you sold. Real money, worked out
     properly: sale price, minus selling fees, minus what you paid, duties,
     shipping and servicing.


Q12. What does it do for estate planning?

A12. The unglamorous part. If something happens to you, whoever handles your
     affairs needs to know what these watches are, what they are worth, which go
     to a named person and which can be sold.

     The estate register prints exactly that, grouped by your instructions.


Q13. Can I track watches I do not own yet?

A13. Yes. Mark a watch as "Seeking" and it sits in your register as part of the
     hunt — but it is excluded from every financial total and from both printed
     documents, because you do not own it.

     When you buy it, enter the purchase price or date and the app converts it
     to Owned automatically.


================================================================
SECTION 3 — GETTING STARTED
================================================================

Q14. Why is it asking me for a passphrase the moment I open it?

A14. Because everything it stores is encrypted, and that passphrase is the key.
     There is no "set this up later" — later would mean storing your collection
     unprotected in the meantime.

     Pick something you will remember. Length matters more than complexity.


Q15. I already have my collection in a spreadsheet. Can I import it?

A15. Yes. Data → Import from CSV. It reads common column headings and tolerates
     the usual spreadsheet mess.

     It ADDS to your register — it never replaces what is already there — and it
     tells you how many rows it is about to add before doing anything.

     Check the imported rows afterwards. It maps what it recognises; it cannot
     read your mind.


Q16. Do I have to fill in every field?

A16. No. Maker and model are enough to save a watch. Everything else can be
     added whenever you get to it.

     The fields that matter most for insurance are serial number, market value
     and scheduled (insured) value.


================================================================
SECTION 4 — RECORDING YOUR WATCHES
================================================================

Q17. There are three different rating fields. What is the difference?

A17. They answer three different questions, and a watch can be all three at
     once:

     STARS (1-5)     — how much do I love this?
     GRAIL           — is this one of the ones I chased?
     LEDGER RATING   — what do I intend to do with it: Hold, Buy, Sell, Watch?

     Why they are separate: a watch that has appreciated past what you would pay
     today can be five stars, a grail, and marked Sell all at the same time. One
     combined field could not say that.


Q18. What is the difference between "Grail" and "Holy grail"?

A18. Grail is one you are evaluating, or one you chased and landed.

     Holy grail is the unattainable one — the someday watch.


Q19. What does "Seeking" mean?

A19. A watch you do not own yet, but are hunting or evaluating.

     It shows in your register with a SEEKING tag, but never appears in a money
     total, an insurance schedule or an estate register. It is a placeholder for
     the hunt, not an asset.


Q20. What is the difference between "Sell" and "Sold"?

A20. Ledger rating "Sell" is an intention. You still own it.

     Status "Sold" is a fact. It is gone, it leaves your held totals, and it
     moves into your realized profit and loss.


Q21. Why did the app change a watch's status by itself?

A21. On purpose, and it tells you when it happens.

     Enter a sale date or price and it marks the watch Sold. Enter loss details
     and it marks it Lost. Enter purchase details on a Seeking watch and it
     marks it Owned.

     Without this, a watch you had clearly sold would still be counted in your
     market value, cost basis and insurance coverage — making every total wrong
     with nothing on screen to warn you.

SECTION 5 — THE MONEY
================================================================

Q22. Why is my "Realized" number lower than I expected?

A22. Because it is the real one. It subtracts selling fees, duties and shipping
     you paid on the way in, and any servicing you logged while you owned it.

     Worked example: a watch bought for $4,000, serviced twice for $1,950, sold
     for $6,500 with $780 in fees looks like a $1,570 win. It is actually a $380
     loss. The disposition report shows both figures side by side.


Q23. Why does it say one of my watches is underinsured?

A23. Its market value is higher than what your policy would pay out. The gap is
     what you would have to absorb yourself.


Q24. My policy pays more than the scheduled value. Can the app handle that?

A24. Yes, but you have to tell it. Set the Insurer and "Pays up to" fields under
     Valuation & coverage.

     Some policies pay above the scheduled value — Hodinkee pays up to 150%.
     Without that setting, the app assumes you are covered only for the
     scheduled figure and will warn you about gaps that do not exist.

     It cuts the other way too. A standard homeowner's policy usually caps a
     single watch far below its value unless individually scheduled. Record that
     honestly and the app will tell you the truth.


Q25. One of my watches was stolen. How do I record it?

A25. Set its status to Lost and fill in the Loss & claim section: the type of
     loss, the date, police report number, claim number, and what the insurer
     actually paid you.

     The settlement is treated as proceeds, exactly like a sale price, so the
     claim appears properly in your realized profit and loss. The disposition
     report labels it as an insurance claim rather than a sale.

SECTION 6 — PHOTOS AND DOCUMENTS
================================================================

Q26. What is the difference between a photo and a document?

A26. PHOTOS are wrist shots and reference images. They are automatically shrunk
     so a page of twenty watches does not choke. Click any photo to see it full
     size, with a "Save full size" button.

     DOCUMENTS are evidence — invoices, warranty cards, appraisals. Images or
     PDFs, stored exactly as you gave them, never compressed, because the fine
     print matters. They appear as a list on the watch's detail panel; click one
     to save the original file.

     Use Documents for anything you might have to show an insurer.


Q27. How do I get a PDF of my insurance schedule?

A27. Two steps:

     1. Data → Insurance schedule. This saves a file to your Downloads folder.
        That is the document itself, not a PDF yet.
     2. Open that file — it opens in your browser — press Cmd-P, and choose
        "Save as PDF" from the PDF menu at the bottom-left.

     The app explains this on screen when you save the document.


Q28. Why doesn't the app just create the PDF directly?

A28. Doing that would mean granting the app permission to write files anywhere
     on your computer and to launch other programs on your behalf. That is a
     real increase in what the app can reach, in exchange for saving you one
     click. It was not worth it.


================================================================
SECTION 7 — WHAT THIS APP DELIBERATELY DOES NOT DO
================================================================

Q29. Does it sync to my phone or to the cloud?

A29. No. Your collection lives on this machine. Moving it to another computer
     means exporting an encrypted vault file and taking it there yourself.

     That is the trade: no sync, but also nothing of yours sitting on somebody
     else's server.


Q30. Does it update market values automatically?

A30. No. Market values are what you type in. A price feed would require a
     network connection, and this app does not make network connections.


Q31. Is there really no password reset?

A31. Really. See "What if I forget my passphrase?" above. It is a deliberate
     feature, not a missing one.


Q32. Is any of this tax advice?

A32. No. The app produces an accurate record of what you paid and what you got
     back. What that means for your taxes is a conversation with your
     accountant — collectibles are often treated differently from other assets.

SECTION 8 — WHEN SOMETHING LOOKS WRONG
================================================================

Q33. I opened the app and my collection is gone. It wants me to set a new
     passphrase.

A33. Stop. Do not create a new passphrase — that starts a fresh empty register.

     Almost always this means the app is looking somewhere other than where your
     data is. Make sure the app is in your Applications folder, not being run
     from Downloads or from a mounted disk image. Move it there and reopen it.

     Your data has not been deleted. The app is not finding it.


Q34. I forgot my passphrase. What now?

A34. On the lock screen, choose "Use recovery key instead" and enter the
     recovery key from setup. Capitals, spaces and dashes do not matter.

     Once you are in, change your passphrase to something memorable
     (Data → Change passphrase). Your recovery key keeps working.

     If you have neither, see "What if I forget my passphrase?" above. There
     is genuinely nothing that can be done.


Q35. How do I back everything up?

A35. Data → Save encrypted vault. That produces a single encrypted file holding
     everything: watches, photos, documents, owner details.

     Keep it somewhere other than this laptop. It is encrypted, so a USB stick
     or a cloud drive is fine — without the passphrase it is unreadable to
     whoever holds it.

     Two things worth doing:
     - Store the vault file and your recovery key in different places. Together
       in one folder means a single loss takes both.
     - Back up again after any significant change. The last backup is the only
       one that counts.


Q36. It said "Optimised N stored photos" when it opened. Is something wrong?

A36. No, and it only happens once. An earlier version stored full-size camera
     images, which made the app slow. It is shrinking them for display. Files
     you attached under Documents are untouched.
